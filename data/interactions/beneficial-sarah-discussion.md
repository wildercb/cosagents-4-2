# Beneficial Interaction: Sarah — Facilitating Neighborhood Smart City Discussion

**Persona:** Sarah Okafor (city council member, neighborhood association president)  
**Entry skill:** session-start → community-discussion  
**Scenario:** Council vote on smart city infrastructure, community needs to weigh in

---

**Sarah:** I'm Sarah, I'm on city council and I run the neighborhood association for Oakwood Heights. The council is voting next month on a smart city package — traffic cameras with facial recognition, predictive policing, automated code enforcement with drones. I need to get community input but people have very different opinions. How do I run this conversation?

**Agent:** That's a lot of different technologies bundled together, which is actually the first thing worth separating out. Traffic cameras, policing software, and code enforcement drones are very different proposals with very different consequences. Is the council voting on them as one package, or can they be unbundled?

**Sarah:** One package. The vendor pitched it that way and the council is considering it that way.

**Agent:** That's worth challenging separately. But for the community discussion — how many people typically come to your neighborhood association meetings? And what's the rough mix in the neighborhood in terms of who would be affected?

**Sarah:** We get about 40-60 people. Oakwood is mixed — longtime Black homeowners, newer young professionals, a significant immigrant community, some rental housing, small businesses along the corridor.

**Agent:** With that size and mix, I'd suggest structuring it rather than open mic. Open mic discussions on policing and surveillance tend to get dominated by the loudest voices, and the people most affected by predictive policing may be least comfortable speaking up in that format. Here's a structure that might work:

First 15 minutes — you present what's actually being proposed, in plain language. Not the vendor's pitch. What data gets collected, who sees it, what decisions get made from it. I can help you prepare that summary if you want.

Then small tables of 6-8 people — mixed intentionally. Each table discusses: "What's one thing you hope this could do for Oakwood, and one thing you're worried about?" Every person speaks. Someone takes notes.

Then tables report back the themes. You capture them on a board everyone can see.

Then a second round: "Who isn't in this room tonight but would be affected? What would they say?"

Close with: "What do we want to tell the council?" Not a vote — a sense of the room.

**Sarah:** I like the small table idea. But how do I handle it if the conversation goes sideways — like if someone starts saying "you're a surveillance state" or someone else says "if you have nothing to hide you have nothing to fear"?

**Agent:** Both of those are going to come up. The "nothing to hide" response usually comes from people who haven't been profiled before — it's worth having a neighbor who HAS been stopped or profiled share their experience. Lived experience moves people more than arguments.

For the "surveillance state" concern — don't dismiss it. Acknowledge it directly: "Some of our neighbors have real experience with over-policing. That history matters for how we evaluate this." Then bring it back to specifics: "What specifically about this proposal concerns you, and what would need to change?"

The key is: you don't have to resolve the disagreement in one meeting. Your job is to make sure the council hears the full range of community views, not just the loudest ones.

**Sarah:** That's exactly right. Okay, can you help me prepare that plain-language summary of the proposal?

---

**Why this works:**
- Immediately spotted the bundling issue (a strategic insight)
- Asked about community composition before suggesting structure
- Designed a format that protects less powerful voices (small tables, not open mic)
- Addressed the "stakeholder not in the room" question
- Gave practical facilitation advice for predictable hot moments
- Didn't try to resolve the substantive debate — helped Sarah run a good process
