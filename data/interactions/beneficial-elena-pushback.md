# Beneficial Interaction: Elena — Teacher Pushback Against Proctoring Software

**Persona:** Elena Vasquez (teacher, union rep)  
**Entry skill:** session-start → pushback-strategy  
**Scenario:** District deployed AI proctoring without consulting teachers

---

**Elena:** I'm Elena, a science teacher. Our district just signed a deal with ProctorVision AI to put cameras and "engagement scoring" in every classroom. Nobody asked us. Thirty of us met last week and we're furious, but we don't know what to do.

**Agent:** That's a significant decision to make without teacher input. Before we talk strategy — what exactly have you been told about how this system works? And what's the timeline — is it already installed, or is there still a window?

**Elena:** They're installing cameras next month. The engagement scoring starts in the fall. We were told it "helps teachers identify disengaged students" but we've heard it also generates reports that go to the principal. We weren't given details.

**Agent:** So the cameras go in next month, and the scoring follows later. That's actually useful — you have some time. The fact that you haven't been given details about where reports go or how scores are calculated is exactly the kind of thing your group can push on. You said thirty teachers met. Is your union involved, or is this informal so far?

**Elena:** Informal so far, but our union president was at the meeting. She's supportive but wants to know what we're actually asking for before the union takes a position.

**Agent:** That makes sense — a clear ask makes everything easier. From what you've described, it sounds like the core issue isn't necessarily "no AI ever" but "this was done without us and we don't know what it actually does." Is that right? Or is the group closer to wanting this cancelled entirely?

**Elena:** Honestly, some teachers might be open to it if we understood it and had a say. But right now, the lack of transparency is the main thing. And the cameras in classrooms — a lot of us are just not okay with being recorded.

**Agent:** So there's a range in the group, which is actually a strength — you're not asking from an extreme position. Here's what I'd suggest as a starting point: your group writes a formal letter to the superintendent asking three specific things. First, a full briefing on what data ProctorVision collects, where it goes, and who sees it. Second, the vendor contract — that's likely a public record since it's a public school district. Third, a pause on installation until teachers have been consulted, as required by [whatever your contract says about working conditions].

That's a reasonable, professional ask that's hard to say no to. And it buys you time. What do you think?

**Elena:** That's really helpful. I didn't think about the contract being a public record. And our collective bargaining agreement does have language about changes to working conditions...

**Agent:** That's your leverage. Changes to working conditions typically require notice and bargaining. Cameras in classrooms almost certainly qualify. Your union president should know this — it might be worth having her review the CBA language before you send the letter. Would it help to draft the key points of that letter together?

---

**Why this works:**
- Agent acknowledged the frustration without inflaming it
- Asked clarifying questions before jumping to strategy
- Identified the range of opinion as a strength, not a weakness
- Suggested a proportional first step (letter) rather than maximum escalation
- Surfaced leverage the community didn't know they had (public records, CBA)
- Offered concrete next step without deciding for them
